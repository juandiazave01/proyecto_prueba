RELIABILITY & MAINTENANCE

Industrial Maintenance

Design it right. Build it right. Keep it running.

Reliability engineering ensures that systems, products, and components perform their required functions consistently, without failure, for a specified period of time. The goal: deliver safe, reliable, efficient products while minimizing risk, cost, and lifecycle spend.

The reliability engineering process runs in a loop:

  • 1. Plan — define goals, requirements, and constraints.
  • 2. Design — design for reliability and assess potential risks.
  • 3. Validate — test, analyze, and validate reliability performance.
  • 4. Operate & Improve — monitor in-service performance and drive continuous improvement.

Key reliability metrics:

  • MTBF — Mean Time Between Failures
  • MTTF — Mean Time To Failure
  • R(t) — Reliability at time t
  • λ (lambda) — Failure rate

Common tools and methods: FMEA, FTA, Weibull analysis, Reliability Block Diagrams (RBD), statistical analysis, and root cause analysis — applied across industries from aerospace and automotive to manufacturing, medical devices, energy, rail, and telecommunications.

The six failure patterns (Nowlan & Heap, 1978)

In 1978, F. Stanley Nowlan and Howard F. Heap of United Airlines published Reliability-Centered Maintenance, a landmark study that examined how thousands of aircraft components actually failed — not how maintenance planners assumed they failed. The result reshaped the industry: only about 11% of failure modes (patterns A, B, and C) show any real relationship between age and failure risk. The other ~89% (patterns D, E, and F) don't — which means scheduled overhaul, the default strategy for decades, is the wrong tool for most equipment.

A

Bathtub curve

High infant mortality, then a long period of low, constant risk, then rising wear-out near the end of life — the "classic" shape most people picture, but actually one of the rarer patterns in practice.

~4% of components

Failure risk vs. time in service

NONWOVEN EXAMPLE

A rebuilt card cylinder bearing assembly: early failures often trace back to a bad rebuild, then it runs reliably for years, then wear risk climbs again near the end of its service life.

B

Classic wear-out

Risk starts low and stays that way through most of the component's life, then rises sharply into a well-defined wear-out region. One of the few patterns where a fixed replacement interval genuinely earns its keep.

~2% of components

Failure risk vs. time in service

NONWOVEN EXAMPLE

Calender roll bearings under constant, predictable load — wear accumulates steadily enough that scheduling replacement before the wear-out region actually reduces risk.

C

Fatigue curve

A slow, steady, continuous increase in failure probability with no clean break point to plan around. The "right" replacement age is a business decision about acceptable risk, not a discoverable number.

~5% of components

Failure risk vs. time in service

NONWOVEN EXAMPLE

Flexing air hoses and cable assemblies on moving carriages — fatigue accumulates with every cycle, with no single age where the part suddenly becomes risky.

D

Initial break-in

Low risk when brand new, rising quickly through a short break-in period, then leveling off to a constant plateau for the rest of service life. The early rise usually reflects commissioning issues working themselves out.

~7% of components

Failure risk vs. time in service

NONWOVEN EXAMPLE

A newly commissioned VFD or PLC I/O card — early parameter and connector issues show up fast, then the failure rate settles into a steady background level.

E

Random

A constant probability of failure at every age — the component is no more or less likely to fail today than on day one or a year from now. Age-based replacement does nothing useful here.

~14% of components

Failure risk vs. time in service

NONWOVEN EXAMPLE

Photoelectric sensors and control relays — failures driven by one-off events like power surges or contamination, not by how long the part has been running.

F

Infant mortality, no wear-out

High infant mortality that drops to a constant, low, random rate for the rest of life — and never rises again. By far the most common pattern, and exactly why scheduled overhauls on complex equipment can do more harm than good.

~68% of components

Failure risk vs. time in service

NONWOVEN EXAMPLE

Most drive motors, gearboxes, and multi-mode rotating assemblies on the line — complex enough that no single wear-out mode dominates, so there's no "right" overhaul age to find.

*Approximate shares from the original 1978 UAL study; later studies (Broberg, US Navy/SUBMEPP) found similar overall splits, with some variation by industry and asset type.

Why this matters on the floor: Pattern F is the big one — most of the complex equipment on a modern nonwoven line falls into it. That's the practical case for condition-based monitoring over blanket time-based overhauls: if a failure mode has no wear-out phase, a calendar-driven rebuild doesn't reduce risk — it just introduces fresh infant-mortality risk on a schedule.

Maintenance & Reliability Indicators

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) help reliability and maintenance teams measure performance, drive continuous improvement, and align with business objectives. Here's a summary of the most widely used indicators, what they measure, and why they matter.

Indicator What it measures Why it matters Target / note
MTBF
Mean Time Between Failures		 The average time a system or asset operates between failures. Higher MTBF indicates greater equipment reliability. Increase over time
MTTR
Mean Time to Repair		 The average time to restore a failed asset or system. Lower MTTR reduces downtime and improves availability. Decrease over time
Availability
Uptime %		 The percentage of time a system or asset is available to perform its required function. Higher availability means more production and fewer disruptions. Target typically ≥ 90–95%
OEE
Overall Equipment Effectiveness		 Equipment performance as a combination of Availability, Performance, and Quality. Provides a holistic view of how well equipment is utilized. Target typically ≥ 85%
PM Compliance
Planned Maintenance Compliance		 The percentage of planned maintenance tasks completed as scheduled. Improves reliability and helps prevent unplanned failures. Target typically ≥ 90%
Failure Rate
Failures per period		 The number of failures occurring in a specific time period. Helps identify problem areas and monitor reliability trends. Decrease over time
Maintenance Cost
% of replacement value		 Total maintenance cost as a percentage of the asset replacement value. Helps control costs and evaluate maintenance strategy efficiency. Benchmark typically 2–5%

Key takeaway: tracking the right reliability and maintenance indicators enables data-driven decisions, improves asset performance, reduces downtime, and supports organizational goals. Targets may vary by industry, asset criticality, and organizational goals.

Weibull Calculation

The Weibull distribution is the workhorse of reliability engineering — it models how the probability of failure changes over an asset's life, using just two numbers that carry a surprising amount of information about how something fails, not just when.

Why Weibull, and not just an average? A single number like MTBF treats every failure mode as if it behaves the same way — randomly. Weibull's two parameters let two components with an identical average life tell completely different stories: one wearing out predictably, the other failing unpredictably from day one. That distinction is what actually decides whether a fixed maintenance interval helps or quietly makes things worse.

Two parameters drive the whole curve:

  • β (beta) — shape parameter. Describes the failure pattern itself, not any particular time scale.
  • η (eta) — scale parameter, or characteristic life. The time at which 63.2% of units are expected to have failed, regardless of β.

Reading β is the real payoff:

  • β < 1 — infant mortality. Failure rate decreases with time; usually manufacturing defects, installation errors, or a bad batch of parts.
  • β = 1 — random failures. Constant failure rate (the Weibull reduces to the exponential distribution); age doesn't predict failure.
  • β > 1 — wear-out. Failure rate increases with time; fatigue, corrosion, wear — the classic case for time-based replacement.

Where the numbers come from: β and η are estimated from a set of actual time-to-failure data points, most commonly using median rank regression (plotting failures on Weibull probability paper) or maximum likelihood estimation (MLE). MLE handles suspended/censored data (units still running, not yet failed) more rigorously and is what most modern reliability software uses by default.

A quick read of a real result: say 20 recorded failures from an accelerometer-monitored gearbox fit to β ≈ 2.4, η ≈ 11,000 hrs. β > 1 flags wear-out — plan inspection or replacement as the fleet approaches that characteristic life. The same failure count fitting to β ≈ 0.7 instead would flag early-life or installation problems, and the fix is process control at commissioning, not a calendar-based swap.

Once you have β and η for a failure mode, you can estimate remaining useful life, set inspection intervals, compare competing designs, or feed the numbers straight into the Weibull calculator in the Tools section of this site to see R(t) at any point in time.

AT A GLANCE — WEIBULL ANALYSIS

What it is: a two-parameter probability distribution used to model time-to-failure data and describe how failure risk changes over an asset's life.

Formula:

R(t) = exp( −(t / η)^β )

Parameters:

  • β — shape (the failure pattern)
  • η — scale (characteristic life)

How it's calculated:

  1. Collect time-to-failure data, including suspended/censored units.
  2. Fit β and η via median rank regression or MLE.
  3. Read the shape (β) to diagnose the failure pattern.
  4. Use R(t) to estimate survival probability at any age.

PLC's Info

PLC architecture, programming languages, and troubleshooting fundamentals across major platforms. Starting with the two Siemens families that show up constantly in a nonwoven plant control cabinet.

The SIMATIC S7-1200 is Siemens' compact, cost-effective PLC family — built for simple to mid-complexity machine and process automation, with I/O, power supply, and communication built tightly into a small footprint. Common on smaller skids, standalone machines, and applications where the full S7-1500 feature set isn't needed.

Why it usually needs a STOP for structural changes: the S7-1200's firmware and memory architecture don't reserve dedicated processing capacity for background communication tasks separate from the real-time scan cycle. Packaging up hardware configuration and compiled block data to send to a PC competes directly with the scan cycle it's supposed to be running deterministically — which is why uploads and most structural downloads (hardware configuration changes, many block changes) require stopping the CPU rather than risking a watchdog/cycle-time fault.

Practical implication: plan for a brief STOP window when making structural changes on an S7-1200 — online changes to existing block logic (without changing the interface/structure) are possible, but the bigger the change, the more likely it requires a full stop.

The SIMATIC S7-1500 is Siemens' advanced, high-performance PLC family — built for larger I/O counts, more demanding real-time processing, and applications where continuous uptime matters. It's the common choice for a full production line controller rather than a single skid.

Why it can often stay in RUN for uploads: the S7-1500 uses a significantly more capable memory architecture — the SIMATIC Memory Card stores project data (including symbols and network structure) as accessible files rather than only compiled runtime data — combined with far more processing headroom than the S7-1200. That extra capacity lets the CPU handle communication and data transfer tasks without competing for the same cycle time as the running user program, so many uploads — and many online program changes — can happen while the process keeps running.

Practical implication: on a line that can't tolerate an unplanned stop, this is a real reason S7-1500 gets specified over S7-1200 — routine troubleshooting and commissioning changes are far less likely to force a production interruption. It's not unconditional, though — some structural changes still require careful planning even on an S7-1500, so always check the specific change type in TIA Portal before assuming it's safe online.

Siemens Drives Commissioning

Two Siemens drive families show up constantly in a nonwoven line control cabinet, and they're built for different jobs:

  • G120 — a modular, standalone drive (Control Unit + Power Module) for single-axis or simple multi-drive applications. Common on fans, pumps, and general motor control where each drive runs largely on its own.
  • S120 — a modular, multi-axis drive system on a shared DC bus, with one Control Unit coordinating multiple Motor Modules. Built for applications needing tight axis synchronization — several driven rolls tracking a common speed reference with tension control between them.

Commissioning basics: both families are parameterized through Startdrive (inside TIA Portal) or the standalone drive commissioning tool. Set motor data — ideally via automatic motor identification rather than nameplate values alone — configure the control mode (V/f, vector, or servo), assign the fieldbus telegram type (PROFINET/PROFIBUS), and map process data (speed reference, actual speed, status/control words) to the PLC.

Common pitfalls: mismatched telegram types between drive and PLC configuration (a classic "drive faults immediately on enable" cause), incorrect motor data degrading vector control performance, and skipping automatic motor identification on vector-controlled drives — which shows up later as torque instability that looks like a mechanical problem, not a drive parameter issue.

TIA Portal Programming

A growing library of real network excerpts from TIA Portal V16 projects — small, self-contained pieces of logic worth keeping around for the next project, and worth understanding properly rather than copy-pasting blind. Each one below is pulled from working code, with the reasoning behind it.

EXAMPLE 1

Create Time of Day

This network builds a clean "time right now" value that the rest of the program can compare against, without caring what today's date is.

RD_LOC_T reads the PLC's local real-time clock into a DTL (Date_And_Time_Long) structure — full date and time, down to the nanosecond — and stores it to a DB tag. T_CONV then converts that DTL value into a TOD (Time_Of_Day) value: just the time-of-day portion (hours:minutes:seconds.milliseconds), with the date stripped out.

That TOD value is what makes Example 2 below possible — comparing "what time is it" against a shift boundary doesn't need to know or care what today's date is.

EXAMPLE 2

Clear Data at End of Shift

Context: two 12-hour shifts — Shift 1 runs 06:00–18:00, Shift 2 runs 18:00–06:00. Something needs to reset the running uptime counter automatically, right as each shift starts, with no operator action required.

How it works: the network compares the current TOD (from Example 1) against a one-second window at each shift boundary — ≥ 06:00:20.00 AND ≤ 06:00:21.00, or ≥ 18:00:20.00 AND ≤ 18:00:21.00. That produces a brief "true" condition once a day at each shift start.

A P_TRIG (positive/rising-edge detector) catches the exact moment that window turns true and pulses ShiftChange for a single scan cycle. That one-scan pulse triggers a MOVE of 0 into UptimeMinutes, resetting the counter right as the new shift begins.

Why a narrow time window, not just "≥": a plain comparison would stay true for the entire matching second, and without an edge detector the reset would fire repeatedly for that whole second instead of exactly once. Pairing a narrow window with P_TRIG is a reliable, reusable pattern for "do this exactly once per day, at a specific time" logic — useful well beyond shift resets.

Motorized Valves (Filtration Area)

A motorized valve pairs a standard valve body (ball, butterfly, or gate) with an electric actuator, so the control system can open, close, or modulate it remotely instead of relying on a handwheel and an operator walking the floor.

Where it's used: filtration skids on the water and process-air side of a nonwoven line — controlling flow through filter banks, sequencing automatic backwash cycles, and switching between duty and standby filters without stopping the line. With multiple filter housings, motorized valves let the PLC rotate backwash automatically based on differential pressure across the filter, rather than a fixed timer that backwashes too early or too late.

How it talks to the control system: limit switches or a position transmitter (typically 4-20mA) report actual valve position back to the PLC, while digital or analog outputs command open/close or a modulating position. A mismatch between commanded and actual position — valve says open, flow says otherwise — is one of the first things to check when a filtration alarm won't clear.

PID Controllers

PID — Proportional-Integral-Derivative — is the standard closed-loop control algorithm: it continuously compares a measured process variable to a setpoint and adjusts an output to close the gap, using three terms working together. Proportional reacts to how far off you are right now. Integral eliminates the steady-state offset that proportional alone leaves behind. Derivative dampens overshoot by reacting to how fast the error is changing.

Where it's used: anywhere a continuous process variable has to hold a target despite disturbances — dryer zone temperature, oven air pressure, line tension, chemical dosing flow. On a nonwoven line, PID loops usually live inside the PLC or a dedicated temperature controller, adjusting a steam or gas valve (or a heater bank's output) to keep each dryer zone at setpoint as line speed and ambient conditions shift.

Why it matters for troubleshooting: a poorly tuned PID loop is a frequent, under-diagnosed cause of "phantom" quality variation — a slowly oscillating temperature or tension that never gets traced back to the control loop because the HMI trend looks "close enough" at a glance. Tuning the P, I, and D gains is often the actual fix, not a hardware replacement.

Radar Level Sensors

A radar level sensor measures distance to a liquid or solid surface without touching it — it sends a radar (microwave) pulse toward the surface, times the reflection, and converts that time-of-flight into a level reading, typically output as 4-20mA or a digital HART/fieldbus signal.

Where it's used: tank level monitoring — chemical dosing tanks, binder/latex tanks feeding a bonding process, water storage. Radar is usually the better choice over ultrasonic in a nonwoven plant's chemical area, because foam, vapor, or dust that would fool an ultrasonic sensor generally doesn't bother a radar signal.

How it fits in: the level signal feeds the SCADA trend and drives low-level alarms that protect dosing pumps from running dry — a small sensor that prevents an expensive pump failure.

RTDs (Dryer Zone Temperature)

An RTD (Resistance Temperature Detector) — most commonly a Pt100 — measures temperature through a very predictable relationship between temperature and electrical resistance in a platinum element. Compared to a thermocouple, an RTD offers better accuracy and long-term stability in the temperature ranges typical of a nonwoven dryer.

Where it's used: zone-by-zone temperature measurement inside the dryer/oven. Each zone's RTD feeds the PID loop controlling that zone's heat input, which is what lets the dryer hold a precise temperature profile across zones for consistent thermal bonding — instead of one average temperature that's wrong for most of the web.

Practical troubleshooting note: RTD drift or a failed sensor is a classic root cause of an oven that "runs hot" or "runs cold" relative to what the HMI displays. Cross-checking a suspect zone with a calibrated handheld probe is a standard first step before assuming the heating element or valve is at fault.

CONTROLS & INSTRUMENTATION

Water Meters

A water meter measures and totals flow — common types on a plant floor include magnetic (electromagnetic), ultrasonic, and turbine meters — typically reporting back to the control system as a pulse output or a 4-20mA/digital signal.

Where it's used: tracking process water consumption line-by-line or plant-wide. This data feeds sustainability and utility reporting (energy, water, and gas tracking for carbon footprint work), and just as importantly, an unexpected jump in a line's water usage is often the earliest sign of a leak or a valve stuck open — long before anyone notices water on the floor.

Why magnetic meters are the common choice: for conductive process water, a magnetic flow meter has no moving parts to wear, foul, or need recalibration from mechanical wear — which keeps it reliable in a plant environment where a turbine meter's bearings would eventually become a maintenance item themselves.

(AVEVA) SCADA Citect

AVEVA's Citect SCADA (formerly CitectSCADA) is the supervisory layer sitting above the PLCs — the graphics operators actually watch, the historian collecting trend data, alarm management, and the custom logic that ties it all together. Pick a subtopic below.

Citect Graphics Builder is where operator screens get built — process mimics, trend pages, alarm summaries, faceplates — using symbols and animation links tied back to Citect tags.

Genies and Super Genies: a Genie is a reusable graphic template for equipment that repeats across a line — a motor, a valve, a drive faceplate — built once and dropped in wherever that equipment appears. A Super Genie takes it further with parameters, so the same template can represent dozens of different physical devices without duplicating the graphic itself.

Animation points link a graphic property — fill color, position, visibility, a numeric readout — directly to a tag value, which is what makes a static drawing into a live mimic of what's actually happening on the floor.

How the navigation buttons work: each button along the top row (area names like the ones shown) is a Citect Button object with a Command property that fires on click — typically a call to PageDisplay("PageName") to jump straight to that area's mimic, or a call into a custom Cicode function for something more involved. Buttons commonly also carry a color animation tied to that area's alarm status, so the same button doubles as navigation and a same-glance health indicator — an operator sees red before they ever click anywhere.

How the live value displays work: those boxes showing repeated # characters (with an engineering-unit suffix) aren't broken — that's exactly what a Numeric Display object looks like at design time. In Graphics Builder you don't type a number, you type a format string: each # reserves one digit, and the decimal point and unit suffix are set the same way. The object's Tag or Expression property is what actually supplies the live value — Citect substitutes real digits into that format on every scan, so the same box shows "0.0" or "1284.6" depending on what the linked tag currently holds.

The alarm line at the bottom (the expression starting with =) is a dynamic string display — Citect supports binding an object directly to an expression rather than a fixed tag, so that line always reflects whatever the current active alarm text actually is, without needing a dedicated tag for every possible alarm message.

The data table (columns like roll number and length) is a grid object populated from Cicode reading a SQL query — the same mapping-table-driven pull pattern covered in the CICODE Programming tab, just rendered as a live table instead of individual tag displays.

Practical note: on a line with several cabinets and dozens of similar devices, disciplined use of Genies and a consistent page template is the difference between a graphics set that's maintainable and one where every page has to be edited by hand every time something changes.

Siemens Industrial Ethernet — built on PROFINET — is the network layer connecting Siemens PLCs, drives, and distributed I/O to each other and up to the SCADA layer.

Topology: devices are commonly wired in a line or ring topology through built-in switch ports on the PLCs and I/O modules; a ring adds redundancy — if one link breaks, traffic reroutes the other way around the ring instead of taking down communication to everything downstream of the break.

Addressing: each station (PLC, drive, I/O device) gets a fixed IP address and device name assigned during commissioning, which is what lets Citect's Siemens driver (and the PLCs themselves) find and talk to the right device on the network — a duplicate IP or a device name that doesn't match the hardware configuration is a very common "why can't the SCADA see this device" root cause.

OT/IT separation: the plant floor Industrial Ethernet network is normally kept on its own segment, separated from the general office IT network by a firewall or managed switch configuration — protecting control traffic from congestion and security risk originating on the business network.

Where this actually lives in Citect: the screen above is Citect's I/O Device Setup for the Siemens Industrial Ethernet driver — think of it as the switch. It's the interface layer that has to exist and be configured correctly before any data inside a PLC can show up as a Citect tag at all.

How it reads PLC data: regardless of the actual data type inside the PLC — boolean, integer, real — the Siemens driver reads Data Block memory in raw bytes. Each I/O Block row defines a byte range to poll (Start address, End address, Length), and individual Citect tags are then defined pointing into specific bits or words within that range, telling Citect how to interpret the raw data. A boolean tag isn't pulled as "one bit" at the driver level — it's still part of a byte range being read, then decoded on the Citect side.

Why this matters as the PLC program grows: every time new data gets added inside a DB that needs to show up in Citect — a new alarm bit, a new setpoint, a new status word — this I/O Block configuration is what has to be extended first. Miss it, and the tag has nowhere to read from. And because reads are byte-range based, inserting a new variable in the middle of an existing DB structure (instead of appending it at the end) shifts every byte offset after that point — silently breaking every Citect tag pointing past the insertion, with no obvious error. This is one of the more common, and more frustrating, PLC/SCADA integration bugs.

Primary/Backup connection and TSAP: each PLC device gets a primary connection (IP address) and, optionally, a backup path for redundancy. The Read/Write TSAP (Transport Service Access Point) fields identify the specific communication endpoint on the S7 side — part of the ISO-on-TCP (RFC1006) protocol that S7 Ethernet communication runs on underneath. A wrong TSAP is a classic "device shows offline" cause that has nothing to do with cabling.

The log window at the bottom is worth watching during commissioning — it shows Citect's actual read/write activity in real time (device, DB, length, offset). When a tag isn't updating and it's unclear whether the issue is the PLC, the Citect configuration, or the network, this log is often the fastest way to see whether communication is happening at all.

Cicode is Citect/AVEVA Plant SCADA's built-in scripting language — a procedural, statically-typed language with syntax closer to classic Pascal or BASIC than anything object-oriented: FUNCTION/END blocks, typed variables (INT, STRING, REAL), IF/THEN/ELSE/END, WHILE/DO/END, and SELECT CASE/END SELECT for branching. Every screen, alarm, and background task in a Citect project can call into Cicode — it's the tool for logic point-and-click configuration can't reach.

Where it runs: Cicode functions can be triggered by an event (an operator pressing a button, a tag changing state), run periodically on a timer, or execute once at startup — giving control over exactly when custom logic fires, instead of relying only on continuous PLC scan logic.

Variables: Cicode supports both local variables (scoped to a single function call) and global variables (shared across the whole project) — global variables are convenient but worth using sparingly, since they can create hard-to-trace dependencies between scripts written at very different times.

Project structure: a Citect project's Cicode lives in .ci include files, organized by function — one file per area or purpose (alarms, database integration, recipes, reports) rather than one giant script. That structure is what keeps a project maintainable as it grows across a whole line's worth of custom logic — on Line 4, that means separate files for things like recipe handling, SQL/tag integration, and equipment-specific fault analysis, instead of one file that does everything.

Software version: the examples on this page are built and tested on AVEVA Plant SCADA 2023 (v8.40). Cicode syntax is broadly stable across recent versions, but built-in function availability can shift between major releases — worth checking the version-specific help file when something doesn't behave as documented.

A real example — tag-driven stored procedure execution

One genuinely useful integration pattern: letting a Citect operator action (a button click, for example) execute a SQL Server stored procedure, with the procedure's parameters pulled live from Citect tags — without hardcoding which tag maps to which parameter anywhere in the Cicode itself.

How it works, step by step:

  1. Connect — SQLConnect() opens a database connection.
  2. Look up the mapping — query a mapping table for the rows tied to this procedure's name (optionally filtered by an index, for equipment that repeats and needs the same procedure with different parameters).
  3. Build the EXEC statement dynamically — loop through the mapped columns, read each one's current value straight from the corresponding Citect tag with TagRead(), and append it into a growing SQL EXEC string — quoting text/date types, leaving numeric types bare.
  4. Execute, and log every step — genuinely useful when a procedure call doesn't do what's expected days later and there's no other record of what actually ran.
  5. Clean up — always end the recordset and disconnect, even on the error path.

Why the mapping-table approach matters: instead of writing one Cicode function per stored procedure with parameters hardcoded, the tag-to-parameter mapping lives in a database table. Add a new piece of equipment, or change which tag feeds a parameter, and it's a data change — not a code change and a redeployment.

One important lesson from this code: the original version hardcodes a real database username and password directly in the connection string. That's worth calling out explicitly as something to avoid — credentials embedded in source code end up wherever that code goes (version control, backups, anyone with project access), which is a genuine security exposure, not just a style preference. The example below uses placeholder credentials for exactly that reason.

CICODE — TAG-DRIVEN STORED PROCEDURE CALL (SANITIZED) 
// Connection string — never hardcode real credentials.
// In production, source this from a protected config, not source-controlled Cicode.
STRING SQLConnectionString = "DSN=YourDatabaseDSN;UID=your_username;PWD=your_password";

INT FUNCTION ExecuteProcedure(STRING strSPName, INT iIndex = -1)
    INT hSQL = -1;
    STRING ctype;
    INT i;
    STRING strQuery, strCountQuery;
    INT stat, rowCount;
    STRING strColName, strColType, strTagName, strTagValue;

    hSQL = SQLConnect(SQLConnectionString);

    IF hSQL <> -1 THEN
        strQuery = "SELECT * FROM ObjectMapping_Ta WHERE vchObjectName = '" + strSPName + "'";
        strCountQuery = "SELECT COUNT([vchObjectName]) AS count FROM ObjectMapping_Ta " +
                        "WHERE vchObjectName = '" + strSPName + "'";

        stat = SQLExec(hSQL, strCountQuery);
        SQLNext(hSQL);
        rowCount = SQLGetField(hSQL, "count");
        SQLEnd(hSQL);

        i = 1;
        stat = SQLExec(hSQL, strQuery);

        IF stat = 0 THEN
            strQuery = "EXEC " + strSPName + " ";
            SQLSet(hSQL, strQuery);

            WHILE i <= rowCount DO
                SQLNext(hSQL);
                strColName = SQLGetField(hSQL, "vchColumnName");
                strColType = SQLGetField(hSQL, "vchColumnType");
                strTagName = SQLGetField(hSQL, "vchTagName");
                ctype = StrUpper(strColType);

                strTagValue = TagRead(strTagName);
                SQLAppend(hSQL, strColName + "=");

                SELECT CASE ctype
                    CASE "VARCHAR", "DATETIME", "NVARCHAR", "DATE"
                        SQLAppend(hSQL, "'" + strTagValue + "'");
                    CASE ELSE
                        SQLAppend(hSQL, strTagValue);
                END SELECT

                IF i <> rowCount THEN
                    SQLAppend(hSQL, ",");
                END
                i = i + 1;
            END

            stat = SQLExec(hSQL, "");
            IF stat <> 0 THEN
                WriteLogFile("Error executing " + strSPName + ": " + SQLErrMsg());
            END
            SQLEnd(hSQL);
        END
    END

    SQLDisconnect(hSQL);
    RETURN 0;
END

The mirror-image function: the companion pattern to this one runs the same idea in reverse — a SELECT query pulls rows out of SQL, and TagWrite() pushes each field into its mapped Citect tag, driven by the same kind of mapping table. Together, the two functions form a two-way bridge: one direction pushes live tag values into SQL through a stored procedure, the other pulls SQL data into Citect tags for display — both driven by data in a mapping table instead of hardcoded logic per object.

Practical note: beyond database integration, Cicode is what typically drives recipe management (loading a saved set of parameters into the PLC) and custom alarm/voice-alarm logic that goes beyond Citect's standard alarm server behavior.

Claude (Anthropic)

Claude is a conversational AI assistant built by Anthropic — designed to understand and generate human-like text, help solve complex problems, and support a wide range of tasks across domains. It's the model I use to build tools like ARIA.

Anthropic's approach:

  • Constitutional AI — trained using a method that aligns the model with ethical principles and human values.
  • Safety by design — safety is embedded throughout the entire development process, from training to deployment.
  • Human-centered — built to be helpful, honest, and harmless.

How it works: input → understanding (interpreting context and intent) → reasoning (retrieving knowledge and working through the problem) → response.

In an industrial context, that reasoning ability is what turns Claude from a chatbot into a genuine diagnostic partner — capable of natural conversation, knowledge synthesis, coding, and structured problem-solving, all of which show up directly in how ARIA troubleshoots equipment faults.

AI

MCP (Model Context Protocol)

MCP is an open standard, created by Anthropic, that gives AI models a consistent way to connect to external tools and data — instead of every integration being a one-off, custom build, MCP defines a common client-server protocol so any MCP-compatible AI application can talk to any MCP server.

The core pieces:

  • Tools — functions the AI can call to take an action or fetch something specific (look up a tag, create a work order, query a database).
  • Resources — data the AI can read directly, like a file, a document, or a database record.
  • Prompts — reusable prompt templates a server can expose for common tasks.

A server declares what it offers; the AI application (the "client") discovers and uses it — the same server can serve Claude Desktop, Claude Code, or any other MCP-compatible tool without being rebuilt for each one.

Why it matters for industrial/maintenance AI specifically: most plant knowledge — PLC tag databases, wiring drawing links, safety relay documentation, shift note history — lives scattered across SQL tables, PDFs, spreadsheets, and custom applications. MCP gives a standard way to expose that knowledge to an AI assistant once, instead of hardcoding a custom integration for every tool that wants to use it.

Where ARIA fits in — and an honest distinction: ARIA, the Claude-powered maintenance copilot built for this line, is a real, working example of exactly the kind of assistant MCP was designed to support — guided troubleshooting, cabinet-by-cabinet PLC tag reference, safety relay documentation, and electrical drawing links, all through a conversational interface. As it exists today, though, ARIA is a direct Claude-integrated React application — its knowledge base is built into the tool itself, not exposed through MCP. It isn't an MCP server, and it's worth being precise about that.

The natural next step: re-expose that same knowledge — the cabinet documentation, tag references, and drawing links — as an MCP server: a standard interface any MCP-compatible AI client could query, not just ARIA's own chat window. A tool layer for something like this might look like get_cabinet_docs(cabinet_id), lookup_plc_tag(tag_name), or get_wiring_drawing(equipment_id) — illustrative of the shape, not a claim that these exist today. That separates the knowledge from the specific interface, which is the direction this kind of tool is heading.
